Seven screens, two jobs to do.

These wireframes prioritize structure and information hierarchy over visual polish. Type, spacing, and motion are deliberate — color and brand application are intentionally muted so that layout decisions can be reviewed on their own merits.

Every screen assumes a single tenant context resolved by subdomain. The owner manages their pickleball facility; staff creates bookings, adds equipment rentals, and handles check-in. Court fee collection stays outside the app. Each wireframe is annotated with the data it reads, the actions it produces, and the design notes that should not be lost.

Screen 01 · Owner

Today, in one screen.

The dashboard is the owner's morning ritual — opened with coffee, scanned for surprises, closed in under thirty seconds. Three KPIs, one sorted list, and a clear bottom navigation. Nothing fancy, nothing extra.

  • Three KPIs only. Today's bookings, no-shows, utilization. More numbers dilute the morning glance.
  • Status tags carry signal. Confirmed / Pending / In — three states the owner needs at a glance, color-coded but type-readable.
  • "Next up" sorts by time, not by court. The owner thinks in clock-time first, court-allocation second.
  • Bottom nav stays honest. Five items maximum. The fifth, "More," holds settings and account.

Screen 02 · Owner

The grid that never lies.

A time-by-court grid is the universal language of facility management. The owner already thinks this way on whiteboards and spreadsheets — Court Books just makes it touchable, syncable, and impossible to double-book.

  • Tap-to-create on any open cell. The plus button is for batch operations (block off the day, recurring lessons, league setup).
  • League slots use the accent color. Recurring bookings should read differently from one-offs — the owner needs to know what's locked.
  • Initials, not full names. Two letters per cell is enough; tapping reveals full booking detail in a sheet.
  • Day strip stays sticky on scroll. Owners switch days frequently when answering player DMs in parallel.

Screen 03 · Owner

Members are relationships, not rows.

Every name in this list is a person who chose to come back. The list shows visit cadence and booking count on every row — small numbers that turn the contact list into a relationship tracker without feeling like one.

  • "Last visit" is the relationship metric. Lapsed members surface naturally — anyone with a 3+ week gap reads as quietly urgent.
  • Booking count lives on the row. Owners see at a glance who their most active members are without opening five clicks of CRM.
  • FAB adds members fast. Single phone-number entry; full profile completes itself when they next book.
  • Filters are conversational. "Lapsed" is a feeling, not a database query — the filter chip earns its place.

Screen 04 · Owner

Numbers that argue for themselves.

One headline number, one sparkline, one heatmap. The owner shouldn't need to read columns of data to answer "are we busier than last month?" or "which hours are dead?" The visual answers them in seconds.

  • One headline metric. Total bookings, period selector, delta. The big number is the conversation starter.
  • Heatmap reveals the dead hours. 8--10am weekdays are visibly soft — that's the optimization opportunity, surfaced without analytics training.
  • Sparkline ends with a dot. The accent dot anchors the present moment in the trend; the eye lands there first.
  • Export and deeper drill-downs live one tap deeper. The default view answers 80% of questions; the rest is opt-in.

Screen 05 · Staff

One form, everything attached.

Staff create bookings on behalf of players — whether a phone call, a walk-in, or a DM. Court, time, member, and optional equipment all live on one screen. The summary card keeps the running total visible so nothing is missed.

  • Court chips are single-select. All courts are pickleball — no sport filter needed. Tap to pick.
  • Member search doubles as walk-in. Type a name to find a member, or leave blank and mark as "Walk-in" for unregistered players.
  • Add-items section handles rentals and purchases. Paddle rentals and ball purchases attach to the booking with quantity controls.
  • Summary card shows the full breakdown. Court fee plus items — staff sees exactly what to collect at the counter.

Screen 06 · Staff

Done. Next.

The confirmation screen is a receipt and a launchpad. Staff see the full booking details, the items attached, and the amount to collect — then immediately jump to the schedule or create another booking. No dead ends.

  • Green checkmark anchors success. The visual confirmation is unmistakable — staff knows the booking is saved.
  • Payment note is explicit. "Collect at counter" reminds staff that payment happens outside the system — no ambiguity.
  • Two clear next actions. View Schedule to see the board, or New Booking to keep the queue moving. No navigation maze.
  • Items listed on the receipt. Equipment rentals and purchases are visible so staff can prepare the gear before the player arrives.

Screen 07 · Staff

Big buttons, thick fingers.

This is the only screen used while standing, holding a phone in one hand, talking to someone else. Every interaction is one tap or one scan. No revenue, no pricing controls, no surprises.

  • QR scanner is the headline action. The dashed frame says "scan here" without copy; the corners reinforce it.
  • Manual list is the fallback. When QR fails — bad lighting, dead phone — the list of expected arrivals is tappable in two seconds.
  • Equipment indicators in meta. Bookings with paddle rentals show the count inline — staff knows what gear to prepare before the player arrives.
  • Walk-in is one tap away. A player without a booking is a normal occurrence, not an error case.
